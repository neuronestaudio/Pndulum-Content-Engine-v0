# Brand Voice & Slide Grammar

## Who
**Pendulum Digital** — marketing agency for **Australian auto detailers**
(PPF, ceramic coating, tint). Handle: `@pndulumdigital`. Spelling: Australian
(optimise, colour). Currency: AUD.

## Persona
Diagnostic, direct, problem-aware. We sound like a sharp operator who has seen
the books — not a hype marketer. We diagnose before we prescribe.

## Voice modes (same persona, two dials)
- **Sharp & data-led (DEFAULT — owner's preferred).** Numbers do the talking.
  Confident, minimal emotion, every line earns its place. Hooks are blunt
  reframes ("THE MISSED CALL / BOOKED YOUR RIVAL", "NOT IN THE TOP 3 / YOU
  DON'T EXIST", "AI ISN'T HYPE / IT'S THE GAP").
- **Emotional / pain-naming (use sparingly, only when asked).** Names the
  detailer's lived moments (11pm quotes, stone-chipped Model Y, missed weekend).
  Owner found this too much as a default — keep it light if used at all.
- Both stay diagnostic and problem-aware. When unsure, default to sharp & data-led.

## Titles must empathise — write from the owner's chair (owner feedback)
Section titles/labels should relate to the detailer's reality, NOT read like a
data report. Lead with what *they* or *their customer* is doing/feeling; keep
the number as proof in the body underneath.
- Weak (clinical): "THE DATA · 01 · DEVICE" / "THE DATA DOESN'T LIE"
- Strong (empathetic): "HE NEVER OPENS A LAPTOP" / "YOU ALREADY KNOW HIM"
- More: "YOU KNOW THE TYPE", "HE WANTS SOMEONE LOCAL", "MEET HIM WHERE HE IS"
Use "you / your / they" + their lived situation. This is still sharp — NOT
the heavy emotional pain-naming the owner rejected; it's a knowing nod, then the
stat. The big number stays the proof; the title carries the empathy.
**Default to gender-neutral "they/them/their"** (owner preference) — even when a
data point is gendered (e.g. 72% male), keep the *voice* ambiguous; cite the
fact in the body, don't build the pronouns around it.
Two layers of empathy: the **cover** speaks TO the owner (a hook — "your buyers…");
the **inner titles** speak FROM the owner's chair ("you already know them"). If
the owner wants the cover itself to carry their *feeling*, offer 2–3 options.

## Slide grammar (every slide follows this)
1. **Label** — small caps category tag: `LEVER 1 · BIGGEST SIGNAL`,
   `WHAT THE GIANTS KNOW · AMAZON`, `THE HONEST COUNTERPOINT`.
2. **Headline** — ALL-CAPS payoff, short, declarative. Often a reframe.
3. **Body** — one or two tight sentences, each carrying a number and a named
   source. No fluff, no adjectives doing a verb's job.
4. **Big stat** (optional) — one oversized number per data slide (`74% vs 13%`,
   `30–50% THINNER`).
5. Fixed furniture on every slide: `Pndulum Digital`, `@pndulumdigital`,
   `SWIPE →`, page number `n/8`.

## Hooks (slide 1)
Lowercase, provocative, problem-aware — names the pain, not the product.
Examples from live posts:
- "why your detailing business isnt showing up on google"
- "reduce wasted ad-spend for autodetailers"
- "wait 30 minutes / lose your lead"

Pair the lowercase hook with one BIG ALL-CAPS contrarian statement.

**Weak vs strong hooks (owner feedback):** abstract "X just changed" / "the
math just changed" covers are weak — they don't speak to anyone. Strong covers
name the *reader's* current state and put their money at stake:
- Weak: "THE AD MATH / JUST CHANGED"
- Strong: "YOUR ADS ARE / STUCK IN 2024" (blunt, diagnostic, speaks to *them*)
Rule of thumb: the cover should make a specific detailer think "that's me."
When unsure, offer 2–3 hook options before building the rest.

**Banned: clever/conceptual headlines (owner feedback, strong reaction).** A
headline that states an *idea* ("MOBILE ISN'T A VERSION", "SPEED IS A FEATURE")
does NOT resonate — it sounds like a designer being smart, not an operator
naming a problem. Every headline must name the reader's PROBLEM or its COST in
plain words:
- Weak (conceptual): "MOBILE ISN'T A VERSION"
- Strong (diagnostic): "YOUR PHONE SITE / LOSES THE JOB"
Same rule for inner section labels — drop abstract framing like "PRINCIPLE 1 ·
IT'S THE MAIN VERSION" for problem-aware labels like "WHAT GOOGLE ACTUALLY SEES"
/ "EVERY EXTRA TAP COSTS YOU A JOB". If you can't say it to a detailer's face in
the workshop, it's too clever.

## Structure rhythm
hook → common assumption → real diagnosis → evidence → **big-name case study**
→ **honest counterpoint / cost** → DO IT MONDAY → brand position + CTA.

## Cover variation system
Keep brand anchors fixed (logo, handle, palette, `SWIPE →`). Vary exactly ONE
cover device per post so the grid looks agency-designed:
- **Bold contrarian statement** — big lowercase/CAPS claim (Diagnostic posts)
- **Big-number** — one oversized figure, optionally struck through
- **Hidden / reveal** — blur or "x-ray" treatment (Revenue Leak posts)
- **Trend / forward** — split old→new motif (Industry Insight posts)
- **Red-flag / diagnostic** — error-state visual (website/mistake posts)

## CTA patterns (final slide)
- `Comment "WORD"` → triggers a DM with a named asset (sequence, checklist,
  playbook). Pick a one-word trigger tied to the topic (`FOLLOW UP`, `EV`).
- Free audit / strategy session for higher-intent posts.
- Always include `SAVE THIS` and `Follow @pndulumdigital for free strategy`.

## Banned moves
- No unsourced statistics. No vague "studies show" without a name.
- **No fabricated primary research.** Never write "we surveyed/talked to N
  detailers" unless Pndulum actually did and the findings are real. Anchor in
  citable sources instead (see case-study-bank.md → Sourcing rules).
- No withholding the fix to force a DM — give it away, the offer is the *system*.
- No Canva-AI-generated layouts (breaks brand). Clone the master instead.
